SERIES | UNEQUAL JUSTICE
In The Governor's Hands

The United States is the only country in the world that condems juveniles to spend their entire lives in prison. In Maryland, California and Oklahoma, the power to release those individuals rests with the governor.



 

By SARA SALIMI and JAMIE KERNER

NOVEMBER 4, 2019



Maryland is one of only three states in which the governor makes the final decision on parole. The other two are Oklahoma and California. No two states could be responding more differently.




CALIFORNIA



While Maryland and Oklahoma have been slow to change their juvenile parole procedures, California governors vowed to change theirs—and largely have. Gov. Jerry Brown took action within months of the U.S. Supreme Court’s 2012 ban on mandatory life sentences without parole for juvenile offenders. He signed Senate Bill 9 in January 2013. That allowed those given life without parole as juveniles to request a new sentencing hearing.



In 2016 the U.S. Supreme Court mad it clear that its 2012 decision applied retroactively. In 2017 Gov. Brown signed Senate Bill 394 which outlawed life without parole sentences. It required that juveniles serving life sentences be given a parole hearing 25 years after being convicted.



Brown’s successor, Gov. Gavin Newsom, went further, vowing in January 2019 to “end the juvenile justice system as we know it.” He proposes moving the Division of Juvenile Justice from the California Dept. of Corrections and Rehabilitation and placing it under the supervision of the Health and Human Services Agency. “Juvenile justice should be about helping kids imagine and pursue new lives — not jumpstarting the revolving door of the criminal justice system,” Newsom said. “The system should be helping these kids unpack trauma and adverse experiences many have suffered.“



In March Newsom suspended executions for as long as he is in office. In September he granted clemency to 21 violent offenders in California. Seven of those had been sentenced to life without parole. Clemency does not free them but allows these individuals to have their cases considered by a parole board. Republicans criticized Newsom saying, “Where is the compassion for the crime victims and their families?”



Newsom’s office stated that the governor considered several factors, including “the circumstances of the crime and the sentence imposed, the applicant’s conduct while in prison and the applicant’s self-development efforts since the offense, including whether they have made use of available rehabilitative programs and addressed treatment needs.”




  OKLAHOMA



The 2016 Supreme Court ruling has had minimal impact on juvenile lifers in Oklahoma, where politicians have a history of being tough on violent crime. At the time of the decision 43 prisoners in Oklahoma serving life-without-parole sentences for crimes they committed while juveniles, according to Tim Laughlin, of the state’s Oklahoma Indigent Defense System.



“It’s not all inclusive, but the ones we identified, at least initially, to the best of our abilities were about 43,” Laughlin said. In 2016 the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals spelled out new sentencing guidelines saying that juries could not impose life-without-parole unless “you find beyond a reasonable doubt that the defendant is irreparably corrupt and permanently incorrigible.”



The legislature attempted to reverse that, passing a bill granting judges the right to give juveniles life with no parole. Gov. Mary Fallin vetoed it.



Oklahoma state law states that a defendant must file proper paperwork to request a lawyer, and public defenders are unable to enter a criminal case and represent a defendant unless a judge appoints them to do so.



“Our statute doesn’t allow us to initiate post conviction applications in non- death penalty cases,” Laughlin said. “At this point, it’s incumbent on the individual to file a post conviction application.”



That has severely limited the initial number of prisoners being resentenced. Only seven out of the eligible juvenile life-without-parole prisoners have been re-sentenced, according to The Frontier. (Links to an external site.) Most still have life-without-parole sentences. Since 2016 no juvenile lifter has been paroled by either of two governors.
