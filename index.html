Growing Up Behind Bars

The United States is the only country to condemn people to life in prison for crimes committed as minors. In recent years the Supreme Court has ruled this unconstitutional. Yet more than 2,000 so-called juvenile lifers remain in prison in what the court says is cruel and unusual punishment.



     


Maryland is one of only three states that gives its governor a say in granting parole. In the 1990s, fear over the rise in violent crime led the governor to declare he would sign no paroles for individuals with life sentences, including those who had committed crimes before turning 18.

The Supreme Court has ruled that juveniles must be given a chance for release. Yet some 300 such Maryland inmates have gone twenty-three years with no parole. That is now being challenged in court in a suit against Governor Larry Hogan.





The story of Maryland’s juvenile lifers and their years without parole was told on the PBS NewsHour by senior correspondent John Yang. The story was researched, shot, and edited by students of the University of Maryland’s Phillip Merrill College of Journalism (Video courtesy PBS NewsHour).

Three Supreme Court decisions have changed the landscape of juvenile justice policy since the "tough on crime" era of the 1990s. Together, they ruled unconstitutional both the death penalty and life without parole for juvenile offenders.

The response to those rulings has been strikingly varied from state to state. Three states had large numbers of juveniles sentenced to life without parole, sentenced to die in prison: Pennsylvania, Michigan and Florida. Pennsylvania stands out for its efforts to resentence and release juveniles.

Three states, Maryland, California and Oklahoma, require a governor’s signature to grant parole rather than the approval of an appointed parole board. Again, those states have responded unevenly. Twenty-three states and the District of Columbia have banned life without parole sentences for juveniles. Marsha Levick, the Chief Legal Officer of the Philadelphia-based Juvenile Law Center, captured the essence of the situation when she said, "In America, it’s either justice by geography, justice by race, or justice by wealth."
