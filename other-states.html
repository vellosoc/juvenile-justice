SERIES | UNEQUAL JUSTICE
Incarcerated For Life

The United States is the only country in the world that condems juveniles to spend their entire lives in prison. Along with Maryland and Pennsylvania, Louisiana, Michigan and Florida harbor the largest juvenile lifer populations in the nation.



 

By DOMINIQUE JANIFER, LAUREN PERRY
and ATHIYAH AZEEM

NOVEMBER 4, 2019



In the 1990s, politicians tried to outdo one another on who was tougher on crime. George H. W. Bush successfully labeled his presidential opponent, Michael Dukakis, as being someone who coddled criminals. In that climate, the United States became the only country that condemns people to spend their lives behind bars for crimes they committed before they turned 18.



Beyond Pennsylvania, three other states had large numbers of juvenile lifers without parole: Michigan, Florida, and Louisiana. None has come close to Pennsylvania’s response to the Supreme Court’s mandate.




  MICHIGAN



Prosecutors and victims’ families in Michigan pressed to keep life-without-parole sentences in place. They pointed to the exception granted by the U.S. Supreme Court in2016, “for the rarest of juvenile offenders, those whose crimes reflect permanent incorrigibility.”



They appealed to the Michigan Supreme Court seeking a path for preserving life without parole. That case delayed action on many of the cases. It wasn’t until June, 2018 that Chief Justice Stephen Markman, writing for the majority said that a trial judge doesn’t need to find any particular fact before sentencing with life without parole. He wrote: “If the trial court simply finds that there are no mitigating circumstances, it can sentence a juvenile to life without parole.”

  FLORIDA



For juvenile lifers in Florida, resentencing is likely only after a long imprisonment. State law and Florida Supreme Court decisions have followed the state’s “tough on crime” pattern.



Faced with a high violent crime rate, in 1995 Florida passed legislation that any lifer incarcerated after that date would have mandatory life without parole.



After the United States Supreme Court ruling on juveniles in 2012, legislators amended the law in 2014. Juveniles who committed murder would be eligible for review by a judge after 25 years.



After challenges seeking individual resentencing reviews, 2018 the Florida Supreme Court put the matter to rest. It ruled that juveniles sentenced to life with the possibility of parole after 25 years indeed had meaningful opportunity for release, and thus are not entitled to further resentencing review.



In June of 2019 Governor Ron Desantis signed a criminal justice reform package that many criticize as being watered down. Still it represents change, if only in at attempt to reduce the state’s massive prison population.



The bill also creates a task force to reevaluate Florida’s entire criminal punishment code, examining whether the set punishments fit the crime.




  LOUISIANA



At the time of the 2016 Supreme Court ruling on juvenile lifers, Louisiana prosecutors were allowed to seek life without parole for juveniles who were convicted of first or second-degree murder. That year a bill was proposed to make juveniles make juvenile lifers eligible for parole after 30 years. It was killed in the Senate. On June 15, 2017 Gov. John Bel Edwards signed the most comprehensive criminal reform package in the state history. It was aimed at shedding the state’s ranking as having the nations highest imprisonment rate.



Prior to the reform package, the criminal reform package gives juvenile lifers an opportunity for parole after 25 years. Facing opposition from the families of murder victims, the legislature amended the bill to allow district attorneys to petition judges for life without parole in the case of first-degree murder.
