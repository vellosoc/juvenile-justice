SERIES | UNEQUAL JUSTICE
Glendening's Legacy

Former Maryland Governor Parris Glendening spearheaded the "life means ;ife" policy that would stay in effect decades after leaving office. He now regrets that decision.



 

By LYNSEY JEFFERY

NOVEMBER 4, 2019



In many places, “equal justice under law” rings false. Justice is particularly unequal in Maryland, a traditionally progressive breeding ground for liberal policy. The Supreme Court ruled in 2016 that juvenile life without parole is unconstitutional no matter when a defendant was sentenced. Yet in Maryland, some 300 juveniles are held in a prison system that’s gone nearly 25 years without a single parole.



Maryland is one of only three states that requires a governor’s signature to grant parole. This system functioned until the 1990s, when heightening crime rates spurred “tough on crime” policies and public demonization of prisoners. Crime and punishment became a hot issue in Maryland in 1993, when “model inmate” Rodney Stokes killed his former girlfriend and himself while on work release, the last step before parole. 



The issue became the center of the gubernatorial elections in 1994. In an effort to better low approval ratings and address the crime issue, new Democratic governor Parris Glendening announced his “life means life” policy in his first year in office. It meant he would never sign parole for lifers. All Maryland governors followed suit until 2019, when current Gov. Larry Hogan paroled a handful of lifers. 



Glendening’s decision delayed lifers’ releases indefinitely, including even those whose papers were already on his desk. “Life means life” is de-facto life without parole for juvenlie lifers in Maryland, though they were originally sentenced to life with the opportunity for parole. The Supreme Court has said that juveniles must have a “meaningful opportunity for release.” The state of Maryland argues that commutations granted by governors over the years meet this standard. But advocates say those opportunities are more like winning the lottery than earning parole.



Glendening has since renounced his “life means life” decision. He’s said repeatedly in public that it was wrong and should be changed legislatively in a potential bill that would completely remove the governor from the parole process. Now, the issue is facing more pressure from a 2016 ACLU lawsuit suing Hogan for denying juvenile lifers a meaningful opportunity for release. The case remains in court proceedings and Maryland’s juvenile lifers remain in prison, waiting.
